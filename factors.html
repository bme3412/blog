Book to Price Ratio

The price-to-book (P/B) ratio compares a company's current market price to its book value.

The book-to-price ratio is used to identify value stocks. Typically, a high book-to-price ratio suggests that a company is undervalued

Earnings Yield

The inverse of the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio and is used to assess the relative value of a company's shares.

Investors use earnings yield to compare the profitability of a company relative to its share price. A higher earnings yield implies a more attractive valuation and potentially a better investment compared to one with a lower earnings yield..

  • Companies may report adjusted earnings that exclude certain expenses, which can inflate the earnings yield
  • The earnings of a company can be highly cyclical, which means the earnings yield may be misleading during certain economic phases.

P/E Ratio

The P/E ratio measures a company's current share price relative to its per-share earnings. It is a valuation ratio of a company's current share price compared to its per-share earnings.

A lower P/E ratio might mean that the company is undervalued, or it might be indicative of a pessimistic future outlook. Conversely, a higher P/E ratio might mean that the company is overvalued or that investors are expecting high growth rates in the future.

  • The P/E ratio does not account for the company's growth prospects, debt, or other factors that may affect a company's future earnings.
  • A P/E ratio can be misleading during periods of negative earnings or when companies have significantly different levels of debt.

P/E Ratio = Market Value per Share / Earnings per Share (EPS)
